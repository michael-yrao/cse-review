## The Two Paths

### Path A: Staff Engineer (IC Track)

Best fit if you want deep technical work, not managing people.

**What "Staff" means at big tech**: You define technical direction across multiple teams, your decisions have multi-quarter impact, you mentor senior engineers, and you drive org-level technical strategy — not just your squad.

**Timeline: 3–5 years**

| Phase | Window | Focus |
|-------|--------|-------|
| 1. DSA + passive system design | Now – Dec 2026 | Finish NeetCode 150. Read Alex Xu *System Design Interview* Vol 1 on Sundays (passive, no pressure). Do NOT interview at FAANG yet — system design isn't ready |
| 2. Fintech-adjacent interviews | Late 2026 – Jun 2027 | Interview at Stripe, Robinhood, Bloomberg, Two Sigma, Jane Street. These companies weight domain knowledge heavily and have lighter/more practical system design rounds. "Design a reconciliation system" is one you've literally built. Use these loops as calibration reps |
| 3. Active system design prep + FAANG | Jan–Jun 2027 | Begin formal system design practice (one design per week, narrated out loud). Now FAANG loops are viable — DSA is solid and design is prepped. Target L5 first, don't hold out for L6 on day 1 |
| 4. Build Staff-level scope | 2027–2029 | Own a technical domain, not a feature. Propose and drive cross-team initiatives. Produce at least one design doc that outlasts your tenure. Mentor 2–3 engineers. Get your skip-level to know your name |
| 5. Promote to Staff | 2029–2031 | Most companies want: (a) cross-team technical influence, (b) a flagship project they can point to, (c) strong peer/skip endorsements |

**Public signal to build in parallel** (any one of these is enough):
- Write 3–5 technical posts on fintech system design — the Snowflake accounting migration is a strong topic
- Contribute to an open-source fintech / financial data project
- Speak at a meetup or conference once a year

**Near-term**: Ask your manager directly what the Staff/Principal promotion bar looks like at Morgan Stanley — the Snowflake/Serenity modernization is exactly the kind of flagship project that justifies that conversation.

### Path B: CTO (Leadership Track)

Best fit if you want to build a company or run engineering at scale.

**Realistic CTO flavors**:
| Type | Realistic Timeline | Notes |
|------|--------------------|-------|
| CTO of your own startup | 1–3 years | You already did Progressive Overflow — do it again, bigger, with funding |
| CTO of an early-stage startup (Series A–B) | 2–4 years | Move to a 20–100 person company as VP/Head of Engineering, earn trust, get promoted to CTO |
| CTO of a big tech company | Not realistic in <10 years | Big tech CTOs are typically former VPs/SVPs with 20+ YOE. Not the right target at 32 |

**If startup CTO is the goal**:
1. You already have 2 direct reports — that's the start of management muscle; now learn to hire and let go
2. Network in the NYC fintech startup ecosystem — Brex, Ramp, Pipe, Mercury, and others have alumni who go on to found companies
3. By 34–36, move to a Series A fintech as VP Engineering or Head of Engineering
4. By 36–40, either get promoted to CTO or co-found your own company

## What 32 Actually Means

32 is not late. Most Staff Engineers hit that title at 30–35. The engineers who feel "behind" at 32 are benchmarking against outliers (the 28-year-old Staff at Google) rather than the median. Your fintech depth — and now your Snowflake architecture work and people leadership — is worth more than generic CRUD years at a startup. The real risk isn't your age; it's staying Senior at the same company indefinitely without forcing a calibration. Interviewing is the fastest way to find out where the market prices you.
